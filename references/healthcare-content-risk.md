# Healthcare Content Risk

Healthcare social media must be evaluated for public trust and safety, not only engagement.

## Risk Domains

Medical accuracy:

- Claims match current guidance or institutional expertise.
- Risk, benefit, and uncertainty are not oversimplified.
- Prevention and treatment advice is framed safely.

Misinformation risk:

- Avoid exaggerated cures, fear-based claims, and misleading before/after content.
- Flag unsupported causal claims.
- Correct myths without amplifying harmful framing.

Privacy and dignity:

- Avoid identifiable patient details without documented consent.
- Avoid exploitative patient imagery.
- Respect PDPA/HIPAA-style confidentiality principles.

Accessibility:

- Plain language for public-health content.
- Thai/English fit to audience.
- Captions and alt text for key visual posts.
- Avoid jargon unless targeting clinicians.

Equity:

- Content reaches underserved audiences.
- Recommendations are realistic for rural, elderly, low-literacy, or low-resource groups.
- Avoid blaming individuals for structural barriers.

Crisis communication:

- Clear source of truth.
- Timely updates.
- Practical next steps.
- Coordination with hospital/public-health channels.

## Healthcare Strategic Value

High-value content usually does at least one:

- Improves prevention behavior.
- Helps patients navigate care.
- Explains seasonal risks.
- Translates research into public benefit.
- Builds trust in institutional expertise.
- Supports recruitment or education without undermining public-service mission.

## Reviewer Questions

- Is this content medically accurate?
- Could it be misunderstood as individual medical advice?
- Does it protect privacy and dignity?
- Does high engagement come from fear, controversy, or public value?
- Does the recommendation match institutional capacity?
